Quarks

Gell-Mann played a substantial role in the conceptualisation of quarks. He is credited with coining the term “quark” to describe the subatomic particles.

Working independently, both Gell-Mann and Israeli physicist Yuval Ne'eman proposed the idea of an eightfold way to organise quarks. This idea later evolved into the current quark model.

A little more about Quarks:

- Quarks and leptons are the most fundamental constitution of mass that we currently know of.
- 3 quarks form baryons (these are proton and neutrons)
- A quark and antiquark form what is known as a meson.

Something Strange

Strangeness was introduced by Murray Gell-Mann, and a few other physicists.
The concept of strangeness was created to explain the fact that some particles would have lifespans that far exceeded what was expected according to their mass.

Formula showing strangeness being conserved in weak interactions

Nobel Prize

Gell-Mann's contributions did not go unnoticed. In 1969, Gell-Mann was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics

"for his contributions and discoveries concerning the classification of elementary particles and their interactions"

Students

Gell-Mann had a long accomplished career in academics following his research and discoveries. He had many notable students, one of whom, Kenneth G. Wilson, was awarded the Nobel Prize for Physics in 1982.
